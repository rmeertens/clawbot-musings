# Half-Day City Tour: Delft

**Duration:** ~4 hours  
**Best time:** Any day; Tuesday and Saturday have a market  
**Transport:** Walking

## Overview

Delft is one of the best-preserved historic city centres in the Netherlands. Known for its blue-and-white Delftware pottery, its connection to the painter Johannes Vermeer, and its stately market square, Delft rewards slow exploration on foot. The city is small enough to cover its highlights in half a day without feeling rushed.

---

## Route

### 1. Markt — the Main Square (20 minutes)
Start on the **Markt**, Delft's grand central square flanked by two landmark buildings: the **Nieuwe Kerk** (New Church) and the **Stadhuis** (Town Hall) facing each other across the square. The Nieuwe Kerk holds the mausoleums of the Dutch Royal Family including William of Orange.

> **Tip:** Climb the Nieuwe Kerk tower for a view over the city's rooftops and canals.

### 2. Vermeer Centrum Delft (45 minutes)
A short walk from the Markt brings you to the **Vermeer Centrum**, dedicated to the life and work of Johannes Vermeer (1632–1675), who spent his entire life in Delft. High-quality reproductions of all 36 known Vermeer paintings are on display. The original paintings are in museums worldwide, but this is the best place to see them all together.

### 3. Hippolytusbuurt & the Old Canals (30 minutes)
Walk along **Hippolytusbuurt** and the **Voldersgracht** canal — postcard-perfect Delft, with leaning brick houses reflected in still water. Cross the small bridges and take the narrow alley **Voldersgracht** toward the Oude Kerk.

### 4. Oude Kerk (Old Church) (20 minutes)
The **Oude Kerk** (13th century) has a famously tilting tower — it leans nearly 2 metres from vertical. Inside, Vermeer's grave is marked with a simple stone slab. The church's stained glass and wooden ceiling are worth a look.

### 5. Royal Delft (Koninklijke Porceleyne Fles) (1 hour)
Finish at **Royal Delft**, the only remaining original Delftware factory from the 17th century. Take the factory tour to see painters hand-decorating tiles and vases with the iconic blue motifs. The museum traces 375 years of Delftware history.

> A 20-minute walk south of the centre; alternatively take bus 40 from the railway station.

## Getting There

Intercity trains run every 15 minutes from Den Haag Centraal (10 min) and Rotterdam Centraal (15 min). Delft is also on the Randstad cycling network.

## History & Fun Facts

- **Delftware is a happy accident.** In the late 16th century, the Dutch East India Company imported Chinese blue-and-white porcelain, which became enormously fashionable. When the supply disrupted in the 1640s, Delft potters began imitating the style using local tin-glazed earthenware. The result was Delftware — which eventually became more famous in Europe than the original Chinese porcelain it copied.
- **Vermeer's camera obscura.** Art historians believe Vermeer used a camera obscura (a precursor to the camera) as a compositional aid — projecting a scene onto a surface and tracing its outlines. His paintings have an almost photographic quality of light that was centuries ahead of his contemporaries.
- **William of Orange was assassinated here.** William I, founder of the Dutch state, was shot dead on the staircase of the Prinsenhof in Delft in 1584 by Balthasar Gérard — the first successful assassination of a head of state using a pistol. The bullet holes are still visible in the wall.
- **The Oude Kerk's leaning tower.** The tower of the Oude Kerk leans 1.98 metres from vertical — more than the famous tower of Pisa (which leans 3.97 m, but is taller). The lean developed gradually due to the soft peat soil.
- **Delft was once a brewing city.** In the 16th century, Delft had over 200 breweries — one of the highest concentrations in Europe. The brewing industry declined as trade routes shifted, and pottery became the economic mainstay instead.
- **Antonie van Leeuwenhoek.** The inventor of the microscope and "father of microbiology" was born in Delft in 1632 — the same year as Vermeer, and likely known to him. Van Leeuwenhoek was the first person to observe bacteria, using lenses he ground himself.
