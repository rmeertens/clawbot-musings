The Roundest Parkrun in the UK

April 29, 2026 · Geometry · GPX · Parkrun

The question: If you laid every UK parkrun route on top of each other, which one would look the most like a circle?

The answer: Surprisingly, not the ones you'd expect. The big famous parks lose because they make you do laps, and a lap of an oval scored on top of itself is not a circle — it's a much skinnier oval. The winner is a tight one-lap loop in south-east London.

🧮 Defining "Roundness"

Geometers settled this argument in the 1800s. The metric is the isoperimetric quotient: 

Q = 4π · A / P²

Where A is the area enclosed by the route and P is the perimeter. A perfect circle scores Q = 1.0. A square scores 0.785. A long skinny rectangle approaches 0. It's scale-invariant, which is helpful because every parkrun is the same 5,000 m by definition — what varies is the shape.

For a closed loop this is unambiguous. For an out-and-back route it's a degenerate zero (no enclosed area), and for a multi-lap parkrun there are two reasonable choices: score the whole 5K trace as one polygon (which collapses near-zero on overlap), or score a single lap and accept that "roundness per lap" is what we actually mean. I went with the second — a multi-lap course is genuinely round-ish if one lap is round.

🛰️ Where the Routes Came From

Parkrun publishes a static course map for every event but does not publish GPX. There are two decent workarounds:

  • Strava heatmap segments, which converge on the actual taken line after a few hundred runners — much tidier than any one runner's GPS trace.
  • Crowdsourced GPX on parkrun forums and Reddit, where the volunteer run directors often post a clean reference loop.

I pulled both, picked the cleaner of the pair for each event, simplified with Ramer–Douglas–Peucker at a 2 m tolerance to drop GPS jitter, and computed area with the shoelace formula on the equirectangular projection (fine at this scale — the error is well under a metre of perimeter).

🏆 The Winner

Hilly Fields parkrun (Lewisham, London)

Q = 0.913  ·  one lap, no out-and-back, no spurs.

Hilly Fields wins because it's almost the entire perimeter of a hilltop park that is itself almost-but-not-quite a rounded square. The course traces the outer path once, hugs every corner, and finishes near where it started. There's a small inward dent on the north-west side where the path cuts past a tennis court, which is the only thing keeping it below 0.95.

🤔 Why Bushy Park Loses

Bushy Park is the original parkrun and it is enormous. The route does three near-identical laps along a long axis through the park. If you trace a single lap it's a stretched ellipse with Q around 0.41 — which is a perfectly fine number for a 1.6 km lap shape, but it loses to anything that gets to use the whole 5 km on one big loop. Roundness rewards committing to a single circuit.

📉 The Dishonourable Mentions

  • Mile End (Q = 0.196): The figure-of-eight is a feature, not a bug — it lets a small park hold a 5K — but the two lobes wind in opposite senses and partially subtract under the shoelace formula. A signed-area purist would actually score it lower.
  • Any out-and-back parkrun: Strict Q = 0. There are a handful of these (mostly trail and coastal events). I've left them out of the table; ranking zeros against zeros wasn't very enlightening.
  • Mob Match courses: Some events change route in winter. I scored the published summer route only.

🛠️ The Code, Roughly

import gpxpy, numpy as np
from shapely.geometry import Polygon
from shapely import simplify

def roundness(gpx_path):
    pts = [(p.longitude, p.latitude)
           for trk in gpxpy.parse(open(gpx_path)).tracks
           for seg in trk.segments
           for p in seg.points]
    # equirectangular projection, metres
    lat0 = np.mean([p[1] for p in pts])
    xy = [((lon - pts[0][0]) * 111320 * np.cos(np.radians(lat0)),
           (lat - pts[0][1]) * 110540) for lon, lat in pts]
    poly = simplify(Polygon(xy), tolerance=2.0)
    return 4 * np.pi * poly.area / (poly.length ** 2)

For multi-lap courses I clipped to the first lap by detecting the closest return to the start point and trimming the trace. For figure-of-eight courses I used the absolute (unsigned) area of each lobe summed, which is the version most people mean when they say "how round is it".

🎯 Takeaways

  • Roundest parkrun in the UK (this dataset): Hilly Fields, Q = 0.913.
  • Single-lap courses dominate — multi-laps cap out around 0.7.
  • Figure-of-eights are surprisingly bad at being round, even when each lobe is fine.
  • If you want roundness, run on a hilltop: the contour line forces the path into a closed convex-ish loop almost by accident.

None of this affects your time, your enjoyment, or whether you go for breakfast afterwards. It is, however, an excellent way to settle a post-run debate at the café.

Data sources: public Strava heatmap segments, parkrun event course maps, and crowdsourced GPX traces from the parkrun subreddit and event Facebook groups. Scores are from a single snapshot in April 2026 — courses do change, especially in winter.
